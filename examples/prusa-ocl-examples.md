# OCL Good Practice Examples

The following examples use [Prusa](https://www.prusa3d.com) products to demonstrate how the OCL license applies in various real-world scenarios.

---

### Scenario 1: Hobbyist modifying and sharing designs

**Background**

Anna, a hobbyist using her printer purely for personal projects, downloads the OCL design files for a fan shroud. She skillfully modifies the shroud design in CAD, making it quieter and improving airflow. It's clearly derived from the original OCL design. Proud of her work, Anna uploads her modified design files (e.g., the STL or STEP file) to a popular sharing site like Printables or Thingiverse. She uses the OCL license for her design.

**License application**

Anna uses her right to modify the OCL design and fulfills her obligation by using again OCL for her derived design.

---

### Scenario 2: Using OCL tools for production

**Background**

Thomas is a business owner, having a small company producing headphones. He uses a 3D printer to print the headphone shells, and a OCL soldering iron-like tool to insert threaded inserts into the plastic to create his product to be sold.

**License application**

Thomas uses his right to commercially employ the OCL tool to create his own product.

---

### Scenario 3: Modifying OCL designs for internal use

**Background**

EfficientRepair is a commercial business specializing in electronics repair using among other things a Prusa toolhead to weld broken plastic. EfficientRepair downloads a OCL design files for a Prusa toolhead and modifies it to speed up its repair process.

**License application**

EfficientRepair uses its right to modify OCL design for its own internal commercial use.

---

### Scenario 4: Selling modified OCL designs

**Background**

CustomTools is a commercial business and downloads OCL design files for a Prusa toolhead. CustomTools modifies the OCL design to create a new "custom" toolhead (e.g., for a niche application). CustomTools then manufactures and sells this new, modified toolhead commercially.

**License application**

CustomTools is not allowed to modify the OCL design to be sold and should apply for business license.

---

### Scenario 5: Repair service relying on a dual-licensed part

**Background**

FixItFast is a commercial business that offers paid 3D printer repair services to the public. Customers bring their broken Prusa printers to FixItFast. Replacement parts necessary for repair (e.g., a specific bracket, a plastic extruder part) have been released under OCL as well as previously under GPLv2. OCL explicitly does not override older incompatible licenses. To perform repairs, FixItFast checks if the official GPL design files are still available and because they are, FixItFast prints these parts using the GPL design files. They then install these self-printed parts onto their customers' machines and charge the customers for the repair service, which includes the cost of the part and labor. FixItFast does not have a separate business license.

**License application**

FixItFast would not be allowed to copy OCL design files to be sold and would have to apply for business license, but because the design is dual licensed under GPL, FixItFast can use the GPL files without those restrictions.

### Scenario 6: Right to repair

**Background**

George is a business owner who bought a stack of 3D printers to produce his products to be sold. His printers are fully utilized and sometimes require replacement parts. George downloads a OCL design files to create such replacement parts which he installs on his printers.

**License application**

George uses his right to repair with the use of OCL design.

---

### Scenario 7: Reselling community designs

**Background**

UpToDatePrint is a commercial business selling Prusa 3D printers to its customers. To have a business edge they want to copy custom community files derived from OCL designs as update parts to be sold alongside the printers. UpToDatePrint wants to keep its obligations under OCL and applies for business license.

**License application**

UpToDatePrint correctly applies for business license; however, UpToDatePrint must also contact the creator of the derived design for commercial license.

---

### Scenario 8: Creator working independently

**Background**

Stephen is a talented creator who likes to transform the design of Prusa 3D printers. He does not use any OCL designs for his creations and works on the printers independently. His creations do not include any OCL components; and therefore, he publishes his creations on sharing site under CC-BY 4.0 license.

**License application**

Stephen's creations that are not derived from OCL designs do not fall under OCL obligations and can be used and shared without the need for further licensing under OCL.

---

### Scenario 9: Prototyping under OCL + RnD

**Background**

Francisco is building a start-up commercial company with the aim to produce plastic action dolls. He's unsure if he can achieve production of action dolls in the quality he envisioned, so he buys a basic 3D printer without the needed capabilities and downloads files released under `OCL v1.1 + RnD v1` for a capability-enhancing module. He builds the module for his 3D printer and uses it for action doll development and prototyping. He confirms that he can achieve the desired quality and buys a separate advanced 3D printer for manufacturing of action dolls.

**License application**

Francisco uses his right under the RnD add-on to make a prototyping device from OCL + RnD files, and then uses a different, non-OCL device for industrial production - keeping manufacturing outside the scope restricted by RnD.

---

### Scenario 10: Forking software under OCL + SWAtt

**Background**

Catherine, a hobbyist developer, wants to make her own forked version of a post-processing tool. The code she wishes to fork has been published on GitHub under `OCL v1.1 + SWAtt v1`. She tracks that the code has been published by the first creator identified as Daniel Hare. She publishes her forked version under `OCL v1.1 + SWAtt v1` and adds attribution of Daniel Hare into the code, and into the tool UI as "Built on the work of Daniel Hare".

**License application**

Catherine uses her right to make a derivative of the OCL + SWAtt tool and publishes her derived creation in a way that fulfills her obligation to keep the license and show attribution in the code and UI.
